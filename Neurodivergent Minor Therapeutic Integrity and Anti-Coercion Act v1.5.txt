Neurodivergent Minor Therapeutic Integrity and Anti-Coercion Act
ARTICLE 1. SHORT TITLE, PURPOSE, AND CONSTITUTIONAL BOUNDARY
Section 1. Short Title.
This Act may be cited as the “Neurodivergent Minor Therapeutic Integrity and Anti-Coercion Act.”
Section 2. Purpose.
The purposes of this Act are to:
1. protect neurodivergent minors from coercive, degrading, aversive, retaliatory, leverage-backed, deprivation-based, communication-blocking, regulation-blocking, performance-conditioning, and materially deceptive treatment practices in covered settings;
2. preserve supportive, developmentally appropriate services that help a minor communicate, regulate, learn, adapt, and remain safe without compelled conformity, deprivation, or independently prohibited conduct;
3. preserve ordinary parent-child conversation, sermons, prayer, and uncompensated pastoral discussion outside covered settings;
4. provide traditional civil and professional accountability for injury, fraud, consent defects, and records abuse;
5. prohibit the use of public funds, public contracts, public placements, and public referrals for prohibited practices; and
6. preserve records, reporting, and oversight sufficient to protect minors and support reviewable public accountability.
7. affirm that the minor’s communication integrity, regulation integrity, and developmental integrity are the primary protected boundaries of this Act.
Section 3. Rule of Construction and Constitutional Boundary.
(a) No viewpoint-only liability.
Nothing in this Act imposes liability based solely on the viewpoint expressed in a voluntary counseling, therapeutic, or treatment conversation.
(b) No speech-alone prohibition.
No person shall be held liable under Article 4 based solely on speech in a voluntary counseling, therapeutic, or treatment conversation absent independently prohibited coercive, aversive, degrading, retaliatory, leverage-backed, deprivation-based, communication-blocking, regulation-blocking, performance-conditioning, or materially deceptive conduct as defined in this Act.
(c) Protected ordinary family and religious life.
Nothing in this Act prohibits ordinary parent-child conversation, sermons, prayer, or uncompensated pastoral discussion outside a covered setting.
(d) Protected supportive services.
Nothing in this Act prohibits supportive, developmentally appropriate services that permit or assist communication, sensory regulation, movement regulation, emotional regulation, adaptive functioning, education, occupational support, or safety support without independently prohibited conduct.
(e) Regulated subjects.
This Act regulates defined coercive or leverage-backed practices with neurodivergent minors in covered settings, materially deceptive factual claims, professional misconduct, records and accountability duties, and the State’s own funding, contracting, placement, and referral decisions.
(f) Construction against orthodoxy.
This Act shall not be construed to prescribe a general orthodoxy of views, to prohibit abstract beliefs or viewpoints, or to regulate generalized speech outside the settings and conduct defined herein.
(g) No claim for disagreement alone.
No civil claim, licensing action, or public enforcement action shall arise under this Act from disagreement with therapeutic conclusions alone, absent independently prohibited conduct, material misrepresentation, or other violation defined by this Act.
(h) What this Act does not do.
This Act does not prohibit supportive services, accommodations, adaptive skill-building, communication assistance, sensory or movement regulation supports, or intervention reasonably necessary to prevent imminent serious harm to self or others, provided such intervention is documented contemporaneously and not used as a pretext for broader coercion, deprivation, or compliance production.

ARTICLE 2. LEGISLATIVE FINDINGS
Section 4. Legislative Findings.
The Legislature finds that:
1. neurodivergent minors are entitled to protection from coercive, degrading, aversive, retaliatory, deprivation-based, and materially deceptive treatment practices in professional, institutional, and treatment-like settings;
2. the State has a substantial and compelling interest in protecting minors from serious psychological harm, coercion, fraud, abuse, and avoidable developmental injury in covered settings, including settings in which institutional leverage, dependency, treatment authority, or compliance-based control is present;
3. content-based speech restrictions are presumptively unconstitutional, and viewpoint discrimination is especially suspect; this Act is therefore drafted to avoid liability based solely on viewpoint or speech alone while still regulating independently prohibited coercive, leverage-backed, aversive, degrading, retaliatory, deprivation-based, communication-blocking, regulation-blocking, performance-conditioning, and materially deceptive practices;
4. the Supreme Court has distinguished traditional malpractice, fraud, and factual-disclosure rules from laws that suppress counseling speech based on viewpoint, and this Act therefore relies on independently provable misconduct, causation, injury, deception, consent defects, records abuse, and related accountability mechanisms rather than viewpoint-only liability;
5. the Legislature further finds that some practices directed at neurodivergent minors do not merely express views, but function as organized mechanisms of compelled conformity, communication blocking, regulation blocking, deprivation, retaliation, and performance conditioning under conditions of dependency and authority;
6. neurodivergent minors may be especially vulnerable when access to care, schooling, housing, support, communication tools, sensory regulation, movement, breaks, food, hydration, toileting access, or adult assistance is conditioned on obedience, silence, stillness, scripted performance, or withdrawal of non-harmful communication or regulation behavior;
7. supportive services for neurodivergent minors must remain lawful when they are directed toward communication support, adaptive functioning, wellbeing, safety, and individualized development rather than coercive compliance production or deprivation;
8. the State also has authority to govern what it funds, contracts for, purchases, places minors into, or refers minors into through public institutions and public programs; and
9. records, sealed minor identities, reporting, and reviewable oversight are necessary to protect minors, support due process, and preserve public accountability.

ARTICLE 3. DEFINITIONS
Section 5. Definitions.
For purposes of this Act:
(a) Minor means an individual under eighteen years of age.
(b) Neurodivergent minor means a minor who is autistic, ADHD, otherwise neurodivergent, or perceived by a provider or program as having traits, communication styles, regulation needs, sensory differences, movement patterns, affective presentation, or behavioral patterns associated with neurodivergence.
(c) Covered provider means a person who, whether licensed or unlicensed, provides or purports to provide counseling, psychotherapy, mental-health treatment, organized treatment-like services, behavioral services, developmental services, or comparable corrective or therapeutic services to a minor in a covered setting.
(d) Covered program means an organization, institution, facility, school-based service, residential program, ministry-for-fee service, telehealth service, online service, contractor, or other organized service that provides or arranges services through a covered provider to minors in a covered setting.
(e) Covered setting means a setting in which a covered provider or covered program provides or arranges organized treatment-like services for a minor.
(f) Primary treatment/correction purpose means that a service is held out through marketing, intake, consent, treatment, program, or comparable materials, or functions chiefly through its actual operation, to diagnose, treat, cure, correct, change, suppress, or modify a minor’s mental state, communication, regulation, movement, affective presentation, sensory response, behavior, or developmental functioning, rather than merely to provide general education, ordinary pastoral care, recreation, peer fellowship, accommodation, or general youth programming. This definition does not reach individualized accommodations, adaptive skill-building, communication assistance, sensory supports, or comparable supportive services that do not involve independently prohibited conduct.
(g) Organized treatment-like service means a service that functions as treatment or correction by objective features, regardless of label.
(h) Objective coverage factors.
A service may be deemed an organized treatment-like service when it has a primary treatment/correction purpose under subsection (f) and four or more of the following factors are present:
1. recurring or scheduled sessions;
2. intake procedures, enrollment, or screening;
3. treatment plans, behavior plans, corrective plans, or analogous protocols;
4. scripted exercises, assignments, drills, journaling requirements, role-play, confession protocols, or progress tracking;
5. supervision by a program, institution, contractor, or organizational chain;
6. payment, billed services, fundraising tied to the service, or organized donation-supported service delivery;
7. institutional referral, placement, routing, or programmatic handoff;
8. claims of therapeutic, corrective, curative, behavioral, developmental, or treatment-like purpose;
9. rules, privileges, housing, access, treatment continuation, educational access, or support services conditioned on participation or compliance; or
10. maintenance of records, files, intake forms, consent forms, schedules, or similar service administration.
(i) No single-factor coverage rule.
No single factor listed in subsection (h), and neither progress tracking nor scripted exercises alone, shall create coverage absent a primary treatment/correction purpose.
(j) Coercive conduct means conduct that uses pressure, compulsion, threats, implied threats, abuse of authority, dependency, fear, shame, or materially consequential leverage to obtain compliance from a minor.
(k) Institutional leverage means the use of actual or threatened loss, denial, withdrawal, reduction, or adverse change in treatment, housing, schooling, placement, privileges, family-access mediation, institutional support, program status, or comparable benefits or opportunities in order to obtain compliance from a minor.
(l) Aversive practice means the use of pain, fear, intimidation, humiliation, deprivation, or other punitive techniques to obtain compliance from a minor.
(m) Degrading conduct means conduct that humiliates, shames, demeans, or requires a minor to perform degrading exercises, rituals, or scripted renunciations, confessions, or performances as a condition of treatment, support, or program participation.
(n) Retaliatory conduct means conduct that punishes, threatens, downgrades, excludes, removes access, or otherwise imposes adverse consequences because a minor refused to participate in a prohibited practice.
(o) Materially deceptive factual claim means a materially false or misleading representation of fact, or materially misleading omission of fact where disclosure is required by law, made in advertising, intake materials, consent materials, billing materials, program descriptions, professional descriptions, or comparable commercial or treatment-related communications, concerning:
1. provider qualifications;
2. the evidence base, safety profile, risks, expected outcomes, or professional endorsement of a service;
3. whether a service is clinically recognized, evidence-based, professionally endorsed, legally authorized, or medically necessary; or
4. any other material fact likely to affect a reasonable parent, guardian, or minor in deciding whether to begin or continue the service.
(p) Supportive service means developmentally appropriate counseling, communication support, occupational support, educational support, behavioral support, psychotherapy, or comparable assistance that helps a neurodivergent minor communicate, regulate, learn, adapt, or remain safe without independently prohibited conduct.
(q) Knowing material facilitation means scheduling, arranging, paying for, transporting to, contracting for, placing into, programmatically routing into, or otherwise materially coordinating a prohibited practice; it includes conduct by a person or entity that materially controls, materially benefits from, or materially coordinates such arrangement. Mere abstract discussion, generalized recommendation, or ordinary conversation standing alone does not constitute knowing material facilitation. Material benefit or control includes financial benefit, enrollment retention, staffing or contract value, placement benefit, referral advantage, or comparable concrete operational gain.
(r) Public funds means any funds appropriated, reimbursed, granted, disbursed, or otherwise provided by the State or a political subdivision, including Medicaid, public education, child welfare, juvenile justice, grant, and contract funds.
(s) State agency includes any department, board, commission, office, school district, public school, public university, county agency, child-welfare agency, juvenile-justice agency, Medicaid agency, or political subdivision of the State.
(t) Communication-blocking act means withholding, disabling, removing, punishing, materially degrading, or conditioning access to speech, augmentative communication, assistive communication, non-speaking communication, movement-based communication, communication devices, communication partners, or comparable communication methods as a condition of access to treatment, schooling, housing, privileges, program support, or other material benefits, or as a punitive response to the minor’s use of such methods.
(u) Regulation-blocking act means withholding, punishing, materially delaying, or conditioning access to non-harmful self-regulation, including sensory regulation, movement regulation, breaks, quiet space, comfort items, hydration, food, toileting, withdrawal from overload, or comparable regulation supports, as a condition of access to treatment, schooling, housing, privileges, program support, or other material benefits, or as a punitive response to the minor’s use of such regulation.
(v) Performance-conditioning act means requiring eye contact, stillness, silence, affective display, scripted verbalization, forced confession, corrective drills, or comparable outward performance as a condition of access to treatment, schooling, housing, privileges, program support, or other material benefits, or as a punitive response to refusal. This definition does not reach short-term, documented redirection reasonably necessary for safety or learning that does not involve deprivation, humiliation, retaliation, or conditioned withdrawal of material benefits.
(w) Non-harmful communication or regulation behavior means behavior by a neurodivergent minor that serves communication, sensory modulation, movement regulation, emotional regulation, coping, or self-soothing and that, at the time in question, does not create an imminent serious risk of bodily harm to self or others and does not physically prevent immediate protective intervention reasonably necessary under this Act.
(x) Prohibited practice means conduct defined in Section 7.

ARTICLE 4. CORE PROHIBITED PRACTICES IN COVERED SETTINGS
Section 6. General Rule.
A covered provider or covered program shall not knowingly or recklessly subject a neurodivergent minor to a prohibited practice in a covered setting.
Section 7. Prohibited Practices.
(a) A prohibited practice occurs when a covered provider or covered program, in a covered setting, knowingly or recklessly engages in any of the following observable coercive, deprivation-based, retaliatory, leverage-backed, communication-blocking, regulation-blocking, performance-conditioning, or materially deceptive acts:
1. uses an aversive practice against a minor;
2. uses degrading conduct against a minor;
3. uses coercive conduct, institutional leverage, or retaliatory conduct to force obedience, silence, stillness, scripted performance, withdrawal of non-harmful communication or regulation behavior, or compliance with corrective demands;
4. conditions treatment, housing, schooling, placement, privileges, program access, family-access mediation, community participation within a covered program, or support services on obedience, silence, stillness, scripted performance, withdrawal of non-harmful communication or regulation behavior, or compliance with corrective demands;
5. isolates a minor from ordinary supports within a covered setting as a means of obtaining or enforcing compliance described in this Section;
6. engages in a communication-blocking act;
7. engages in a regulation-blocking act;
8. requires a performance-conditioning act as a condition of continued access to treatment, housing, schooling, placement, privileges, or program support;
9. deprives a minor of communication tools, assistive devices, comfort items, sensory supports, movement, breaks, food, hydration, toileting access, or human contact as a means of obtaining compliance, except where reasonably necessary to prevent imminent serious harm to self or others, including short-term documented de-escalation or removal from an overstimulating environment, documented contemporaneously, and not used as a pretext for broader coercion, deprivation, or compliance production; or
10. knowingly or recklessly makes or causes to be made a materially deceptive factual claim in connection with services to the minor.
(b) A prohibited practice also occurs when a covered provider or covered program knowingly materially facilitates conduct prohibited by this Section.
Section 8. No Liability Based on Viewpoint Alone; Observable-Acts Construction.
(a) No liability under this Article shall arise solely because a provider or program expressed a viewpoint in a voluntary counseling, therapeutic, or treatment conversation.
(b) Article 4 shall be construed to prohibit observable coercive, deprivation-based, retaliatory, leverage-backed, communication-blocking, regulation-blocking, performance-conditioning, or materially deceptive acts in covered settings, and not to impose liability merely because a service addresses neurodivergent traits, behavior, communication, or regulation.
(c) Liability under this Article requires independently prohibited conduct under Section 7.
Section 9. No Label-Based Evasion.
A service shall not escape coverage under this Article solely because it is labeled mentoring, coaching, discipline, behavior support, social-skills training, character formation, ministry, peer support, therapy readiness, school readiness, educational support, or any similar term, if the service otherwise functions as an organized treatment-like service under Section 5.
Section 10. No Payment Requirement.
The absence of formal payment does not by itself remove a service from coverage if the service otherwise qualifies as an organized treatment-like service.

ARTICLE 5. EXCLUSIONS AND SAFE HARBORS
Section 11. Exclusions.
This Act does not prohibit, without more:
1. ordinary parent-child conversation outside a covered setting;
2. sermons, prayer, religious instruction, or uncompensated pastoral discussion outside a covered setting;
3. a minor’s own decision to discuss values, behavior, distress, identity, goals, or relationships;
4. supportive services;
5. individualized educational accommodations, communication supports, sensory supports, occupational supports, adaptive supports, or related assistance that do not involve independently prohibited conduct;
6. intervention reasonably necessary to prevent imminent serious harm to self or others, including short-term documented de-escalation or removal from an overstimulating environment, provided it is documented contemporaneously and not used as a pretext for broader coercion, deprivation, or compliance production; or
7. neutral enforcement of generally applicable rules against threats, violence, or unlawful conduct, provided such rules are not used as a pretext for prohibited practices.
Section 12. Good-Faith Supportive Services Safe Harbor.
A provider does not violate this Act by engaging in good-faith, developmentally appropriate services that help a neurodivergent minor communicate, regulate, adapt, learn, or remain safe, so long as the provider does not engage in prohibited conduct under Article 4, make materially deceptive factual claims, or otherwise violate Articles 6 through 9.

ARTICLE 6. CIVIL REMEDIES
Section 13. Private Right of Action.
A person subjected to a prohibited practice while a minor may bring a civil action against:
1. the covered provider;
2. the covered program;
3. any employer, supervisor, contractor, institution, or entity that authorized, directed, knowingly facilitated, materially coordinated, materially benefited from, or recklessly failed to prevent the prohibited practice; and
4. any person or entity that knowingly or recklessly made or profited from a materially deceptive factual claim connected to services for the minor.
Section 14. Elements.
(a) A claim under this Article based on professional breach, negligence, or other misconduct shall require proof of:
1. a covered provider or covered program relationship, or another comparable duty-producing relationship recognized by law;
2. breach of duty or other independently actionable misconduct under this Act or other law;
3. causation;
4. actual injury; and
5. where applicable, any additional element required by governing state law.
(b) A claim under this Article based on materially deceptive factual claims shall require proof of:
1. scienter, meaning that the defendant knowingly or recklessly made or caused to be made the materially deceptive factual claim;
2. materiality;
3. actual reliance by the parent, guardian, or minor to the extent required by applicable state law;
4. injury; and
5. any additional element required by applicable state consumer-protection, fraud, or misrepresentation law.
Section 15. Available Relief.
A court may award:
1. actual damages;
2. statutory damages of not less than [insert amount] per violation or course of prohibited practice;
3. punitive damages where otherwise authorized by state law;
4. injunctive relief;
5. declaratory relief;
6. attorney fees and costs to a prevailing plaintiff where authorized by state law or this Act; and
7. any other relief authorized by law.
Section 16. Limitations Period.
An action under this Article may be commenced not later than the later of:
1. [insert number] years after the plaintiff reaches eighteen years of age; or
2. [insert number] years after the plaintiff discovers, or reasonably should have discovered, the injury and its connection to the prohibited practice.
The limitations period shall be tolled during any period of fraudulent concealment, intimidation, legal incapacity, or other equitable tolling recognized by state law.
Section 17. No Displacement of Other Claims.
A claim under this Act does not preclude any other claim available under state law, including malpractice, negligence, consumer protection, fraud, battery, assault, negligent misrepresentation, breach of fiduciary duty, disability discrimination, denial of accommodation, or records-based claims.

ARTICLE 7. CONSUMER PROTECTION AND PROFESSIONAL ACCOUNTABILITY
Section 18. Consumer Protection.
A materially deceptive factual claim connected to services for a minor shall constitute an unfair or deceptive act or practice under [State Consumer Protection Act] to the extent authorized by that Act.
Section 19. Unprofessional Conduct.
For any licensed profession regulated by the State, a violation of this Act constitutes unprofessional conduct when based on:
1. independently prohibited conduct under Article 4;
2. materially deceptive factual claims;
3. consent failures required by law;
4. records destruction, concealment, or falsification;
5. retaliation;
6. knowing material facilitation of prohibited conduct; or
7. breach of the applicable professional standard of care.
Section 20. No Discipline Based on Abstract Viewpoint or Supportive Services Alone.
(a) No licensing board may impose discipline under this Act based solely on a provider’s abstract views, beliefs, or generalized speech outside a covered setting.
(b) No licensing board may impose discipline under this Act solely because a provider offered supportive services consistent with Section 12, absent independently prohibited conduct, materially deceptive factual claims, consent defects, records violations, or another independently provable breach recognized by this Act or other law.
(c) Licensing discipline under this Act shall require the same independent proof of prohibited conduct, materially deceptive factual claims, consent defects, records violations, or other provable breach that would support liability or discipline under the applicable section.
Section 21. Board Authority.
The applicable [Licensing Board] may investigate complaints, subpoena records as authorized by law, require corrective action, order supervision or training, suspend or revoke licenses, impose discipline, and refer matters for civil or criminal enforcement where independently appropriate.

ARTICLE 8. RECORDS, NOTICE, AND EVIDENTIARY SAFEGUARDS
Section 22. Record Retention.
A covered provider or covered program serving minors shall preserve for not less than [insert number] years after the minor reaches the age of majority:
1. intake materials;
2. consent materials;
3. treatment plans, corrective plans, or analogous protocols;
4. written exercises, assignments, scripts, drills, or program instructions;
5. billing and payment records;
6. referral and placement records;
7. advertisements and marketing materials;
8. session notes or comparable records, subject to privacy law; and
9. communications or records otherwise required by law.
Section 23. Procedural Notice.
A covered provider or covered program serving minors shall provide factual, non-ideological written notice that:
1. this State prohibits certain coercive, aversive, degrading, retaliatory, leverage-backed, deprivation-based, communication-blocking, regulation-blocking, performance-conditioning, and materially deceptive practices with neurodivergent minors;
2. complaints may be filed with [Department] and relevant licensing boards;
3. records are subject to preservation requirements under state law;
4. minor identities are protected as provided by law; and
5. the notice is procedural only and does not itself constitute endorsement of any therapeutic conclusion.
Section 24. Destruction, Concealment, or Falsification of Records.
Knowingly destroying, concealing, materially altering, or falsifying records relevant to a complaint, investigation, licensing matter, or civil action under this Act is a separate violation and may support civil penalties, adverse evidentiary inferences, professional discipline, and any other sanction authorized by law.
Section 25. Adverse Inferences.
Where a covered provider or covered program was under a duty to preserve records under this Act and knowingly failed to do so, the court or tribunal may impose adverse evidentiary inferences and any other appropriate remedy authorized by law.
Section 26. Sealed Minor Identities.
Any record or proceeding under this Act shall protect minor identities and medical, mental-health, developmental, and disability-related information as required by law. Public disclosures shall be de-identified except as otherwise authorized by law.

ARTICLE 9. PUBLIC FUNDING, PROCUREMENT, PLACEMENT, AND REFERRAL
Section 27. Public Funding Restriction.
No public funds shall be used to pay for, reimburse, subsidize, grant, or contract for a prohibited practice.
Section 28. Medicaid and Public Insurance.
The [State Medicaid Agency/Public Insurance Agency] shall not reimburse prohibited practices and shall adopt billing, auditing, and recoupment rules necessary to enforce this Section.
Section 29. Public Referral and Placement Restriction.
No state agency, public school, public university, child-welfare agency, juvenile-justice agency, or publicly funded contractor shall knowingly materially facilitate, refer a minor into, or place a minor into a prohibited practice.
Section 30. Contracting and Procurement Restriction.
A state agency shall not enter into, renew, or maintain a contract with a person or entity that knowingly performs, arranges, materially coordinates, or materially facilitates prohibited practices for minors.
Section 31. Foster Care, Residential Care, and Juvenile Settings.
A foster-placement provider, residential facility, detention facility, or publicly funded program shall not subject a minor to a prohibited practice.

ARTICLE 10. ENTERPRISE LIABILITY AND ANTI-EVASION
Section 32. Coordinated Enterprise Liability.
Where multiple persons or entities materially coordinate a common course of prohibited practice through shared supervision, organizational control, placement, routing, contracting, records, operational planning, or material benefit, liability and discipline under this Act may attach across the coordinated enterprise as permitted by law.
Section 33. No Evasion by Fragmentation.
No person or entity may evade this Act by dividing roles among affiliates, volunteers, contractors, ministries, nonprofits, schools, vendors, or out-of-state programs where that person or entity materially coordinates, materially controls, materially benefits from, or knowingly materially facilitates the prohibited practice.
Section 34. Anti-Waiver.
To the extent permitted by state and federal law, rights, duties, and protections under this Act may not be waived by private agreement.

ARTICLE 12. TRACK-SPECIFIC FINDINGS AND FALLBACK OPERATION
Section 39. Track-Specific Legislative Findings.
(a) Article 4 findings.
The Legislature finds that neurodivergent minors are especially vulnerable to coercive, aversive, degrading, retaliatory, leverage-backed, deprivation-based, communication-blocking, regulation-blocking, and performance-conditioning practices in covered settings and that the State may regulate such practices without imposing liability based solely on viewpoint.
(b) Articles 6 and 7 findings.
The Legislature finds that materially deceptive factual claims, professional breach, consent defects, and records abuse are independently actionable under traditional civil and professional-accountability principles.
(c) Articles 8 through 11 findings.
The Legislature finds that records, procedural notice, sealed identities, reporting, public-funding limits, procurement limits, public placements, referrals, and coordinated-enterprise rules are independently necessary to protect minors and preserve reviewable public accountability.
Section 40. Fallback Operation.
(a) If any provision of Article 4 is held invalid as applied to speech alone, Article 4 shall continue to apply to nonexpressive coercive conduct, aversive methods, degrading conduct, retaliation, institutional leverage, condition-based practices, communication-blocking acts, regulation-blocking acts, deprivation-based practices, performance-conditioning acts, and knowing material facilitation to the maximum extent permitted by law.
(b) Articles 6 through 11 remain independently operative unless separately held invalid.
(c) Nothing in this Section authorizes liability contrary to the constitutional-boundary rules in Article 1.

ARTICLE 13. INTERNAL SUMMARY, SEVERABILITY, AND EFFECTIVE DATE
Section 41. Internal Summary.
This Act protects neurodivergent minors from coercive, degrading, aversive, retaliatory, leverage-backed, deprivation-based, communication-blocking, regulation-blocking, performance-conditioning, and materially deceptive treatment practices; preserves ordinary family and religious life and supportive services without independently prohibited conduct; creates traditional civil and professional accountability for injury, fraud, consent defects, and records abuse; and governs public funding, placement, procurement, and reporting for the protection of minors.
Section 42. Severability.
If any provision of this Act or its application to any person or circumstance is held invalid, that invalidity does not affect any other provision or application that can be given effect without the invalid provision or application, and to this end the provisions of this Act are severable.
Section 43. Effective Date.
This Act takes effect on [insert date].
